Autonomy & Empowerment

I read Elizabeth Ayer’s article on signaling intent that you shared and then I started snooping around the rest of her articles on medium. She had one on empowering people that is particularly pertinent to a couple of conversations that we’ve had.

This article does a great job of outlining how a coach can move someone up the ladder of empowerment and autonomy so that they can reach a level of self-actualization.

There are several dichotomies of leadership and this is one of them: you have to give people autonomy but at the same time you don't want to abandon them. I've seen a few leaders get this wrong: they were almost completely absent and didn't provide any direction (in the name of autonomy) and, as a result, the individuals and the team's performance suffered.

It's the job of a coach to give them the support that they need and, at low levels of TRM, this includes telling people what to do all the while coaching them to a level where they don't need as much support. When you don't know what you don't know, you need a bit of direction.

I feel that too often, myself included, people get stuck in an all-or-nothing approach to autonomy but that is neither practical nor does it work when it isn't executed in a graduated manner.

I've heard a few different ideas about what autonomy and management ideals mean around the office but it seems that there's some confusion as to what that actually looks like.

I get the feeling, from some of the conversations that I’ve heard around the office, that people here feel like autonomy is an all or nothing thing: that you shouldn't tell anybody what to do. But this breaks down in a number of different circumstances. The most notable of which is the low TRM employee who doesn't know what they don't know.

We talked about that situation and how you can give them something to work on that stretches them.

That, when the person has low TRM, includes telling them what to do since Telling people what to do and autonomy are not mutually exclusive when used correctly in a coaching/mentoring context.
